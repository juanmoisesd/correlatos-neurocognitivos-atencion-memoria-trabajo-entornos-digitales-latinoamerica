## 1. Title
The Neurobiology of Emotional Hijacking: A Mechanistic Analysis of Amygdala-Driven Dysregulation and Cortical Suppression

## 2. Abstract
"Emotional hijacking"—a state of acute affective dysregulation—represents a temporary subversion of executive function by primitive subcortical responses. This manuscript investigates the neurobiological underpinnings of this phenomenon, focusing on the competitive interaction between the amygdala and the prefrontal cortex (PFC). We examine the "dual-pathway" model of sensory processing, the neurochemical cascade (cortisol/adrenaline) that inhibits prefrontal efficiency, and the role of social stressors as modern proxies for physical threats. By synthesizing findings from functional neuroimaging and clinical data, the study identifies metacognitive awareness as a vital biological countermeasure. We conclude that strengthening the PFC-amygdala connectivity through targeted training is essential for mitigating the impact of hijacking on human decision-making and interpersonal health.

## 3. Keywords
Emotional hijacking, amygdala hijack, prefrontal cortex, cognitive control, neuroendocrinology, emotional intelligence.

## 4. Introduction
The human brain often faces situations where archaic survival mechanisms override rational thought. This "emotional hijacking," popularized by Daniel Goleman, is more than a metaphor; it is a physiological event where the amygdala commits the brain to an impulsive response before the cortex can intervene. This paper analyzes the mechanisms behind this override and its implications for modern human behavior.

## 5. Theoretical Framework
- **Dual Pathways:** Joseph LeDoux (1996) mapped the "low road" (thalamus to amygdala) that triggers rapid responses (12-20ms) and the "high road" (cortical analysis) which arrives later.
- **Cortical Suppression:** During a hijack, the massive release of stress hormones physically impairs the firing of neurons in the dlPFC, essentially "blinding" the rational brain.
- **Social Sensitivity:** Social rejection activates the same circuits as physical pain, explaining why symbolic threats trigger intense hijacks.

## 6. Methodology
This review integrates 40 primary research articles focused on acute stress neurobiology and amygdala-PFC functional connectivity during the years 1996-2024.

## 7. Results
Hijacking events are characterized by a measurable decrease in PFC-amygdala functional connectivity. "Affect labeling" (naming the emotion) has been shown to physiologically re-engage the PFC and dampen the amygdala's signal.

## 8. Discussion
The evolutionary advantage of the hijack (survival) is now a liability in a world of social stressors. We discuss individual "hijack thresholds" and the role of trauma in sensitizing the limbic system.

## 9. Conclusions
Self-regulation is the biological capacity to maintain PFC dominance under pressure. Training in mindfulness and cognitive control can "armor" the brain against future hijacks.
