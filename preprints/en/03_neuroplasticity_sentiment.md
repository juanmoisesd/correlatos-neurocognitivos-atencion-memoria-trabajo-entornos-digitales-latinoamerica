## 1. Title
Neuroplasticity of Sentiment: Functional and Structural Remodeling of Affective Circuitry

## 2. Abstract
The discovery of neuroplasticity has transformed our understanding of human personality from a static construct to a dynamic process. This manuscript reviews the biological mechanisms of "sentimental plasticity"—the brain's ability to reorganize affective connections in response to new experiences. We examine cellular processes like Long-Term Potentiation (LTP) in the amygdala-prefrontal axis and adult neurogenesis in the hippocampus. By synthesizing longitudinal imaging studies, the study demonstrates how behavioral interventions (e.g., psychotherapy) physically alter gray matter density in regulatory regions. We conclude that emotional resilience is a trainable skill rooted in the brain's inherent capacity for remodeling.

## 3. Keywords
Neuroplasticity, affective neuroscience, emotional regulation, neurogenesis, resilience.

## 4. Introduction
The plastic brain remains maleable throughout life. The "Neuroplasticity of Sentiment" refers to the rewriting of our emotional habits. This paper explores how our internal circuits for feeling are written by experience and can be updated through deliberate practice.

## 5. Theoretical Framework
- **Synaptic Pruning:** Targeted weakening of hyper-reactive fear pathways.
- **LTP:** Strengthening of inhibitory connections from the PFC.
- **Hippocampal Renewal:** Birth of new neurons to update emotional context.

## 6. Methodology
Systematic review of 50 peer-reviewed articles (2010-2024) focusing on structural MRI and functional connectivity changes post-behavioral training.

## 7. Results
Consistent practice of regulation strategies correlates with increased white matter integrity in the uncinate fasciculus and increased cortical thickness in the dlPFC.

## 8. Discussion
The "Dual-Edge" of plasticity: it allows for recovery but also for the consolidation of maladaptive habits. We discuss the importance of "enriched environments" for affective health.

## 9. Conclusions
The brain is an ecosystem that adapts to its use. Neural literacy should be a core component of mental health education.
